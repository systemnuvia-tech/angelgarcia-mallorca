Understanding vital signs — and why a single reading says nothing.

Angel GarciaICU nurse · 20+ years7 min read

Heart rate, oxygen saturation, activity — modern wearables measure a great deal today. But numbers alone do not reassure; they can even worry you needlessly. The real value only emerges through interpretation: knowing what is normal, what is only a moment — and what is a trend you should take seriously.

1. The key vital signs — briefly explained

Heart rate · pulse

How often the heart beats per minute. Naturally varies with movement, excitement and rest.

Oxygen saturation · SpO₂

How well the blood is supplied with oxygen. Always viewed in context, never as a single figure.

Activity · movement

How active the day is. Changes in the pattern are often more telling than any single day.

Rest · recovery

Rest phases and their quality. Markedly more or less rest than usual can be a sign.

As a rough orientation, the resting pulse in many adults lies between about 60 and 100 beats per minute. But — and this is the point — "normal" is individual. What is usual for one person may be notable in another. What is reliable is not the table, but the comparison with one's own normality.

2. Single reading vs. trend: the decisive difference

A single reading is a snapshot — and moments sometimes lie. Someone who has just had a fright has a higher pulse. Someone in deep sleep, a lower one. On its own, that says little.

What becomes meaningful is a course: the same direction over hours. An oxygen saturation slowly drifting. A resting pulse staying unusually high across the day. Markedly less activity than in the weeks before. Such trends are the real early warning — not the single outlier.

3. The staircase example

A person climbs the stairs. The pulse rises — that is healthy and right. A pure alarm device might go off here. Anyone with experience knows: right afterwards, the pulse settles again. No cause for concern.

It is different when the same raised pulse persists for no reason — over one, two, three hours, at rest. Exactly this distinction — normal exertion with recovery versus a persistent pattern — separates panic from real early detection.

To be honest: This is not magic and not a diagnosis. It is experience. More than twenty years of intensive-care and anaesthesia nursing mean having seen, interpreted and acted on exactly such courses countless times — before anything escalated.

4. Vital signs and fall prevention

Falls are among the greatest risks in old age. A wearable does not prevent a fall — but changes in the pattern can give clues: declining activity, more restlessness, suddenly far more rest phases, an irregular daily rhythm. Such signals are taken seriously, observed and, if needed, measures are discussed early — from home safety to a talk with the doctor. Here too: the reading is the beginning, the interpretation is everything.

5. Why the person behind the data matters

Technology can make signals visible. It cannot know whether someone has just taken the stairs, whether a reading fits the person or falls outside their range. That is the task of an experienced eye.
